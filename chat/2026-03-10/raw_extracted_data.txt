Mark P never gave a direct answer, but do you think baseball is better or worse off since I created baseball-related sabermetrics and algorithms? SPEAKER: Steve Adams
---
I didn’t see Mark’s reply, but it’s a subjective question so of course there was no direct answer. If Bill James didn’t pioneer so much of the incorporation of data into baseball decision-making, someone else would have. The arrival of more sophisticated data to evaluate players was a foregone conclusion. Even fans who like things like pitcher wins/losses can acknowledge how flawed the stats are. A pitcher “win” can include 8 shutout innings and 16 strikeouts or 5 1/3 innings with 7 runs allowed — but on a day when your mound opponent was torched for 9 runs … someone was always going to come along and say “This is silly. Just like it’s silly to penalize a pitcher with an ‘earned’ run because his second baseman lost a pop-up in the sun” I grew up watching baseball in the 80s and 90s but love the influx of data in the modern game. The game itself remains fun to watch regardless or what information I’m using to determine player values and such. I just like baseball.
